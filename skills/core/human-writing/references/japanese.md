# Japanese — AI tells and repairs

Load this when drafting or reviewing a Japanese-language piece. Judge by
accumulation, not single occurrences.

## Style-consistency tells

- です・ます and だ・である mixed in one piece. Choose by genre — です・ます
  for essays, blogs, and announcements; だ・である for papers, reports, and
  opinionated columns — and hold it throughout. Mixing is the single
  strongest generated-text signal in Japanese.
- Over-explicit subjects: 私は/私たちは repeated where Japanese omits the
  recoverable subject. Repair: drop the subject unless its return marks a
  contrast.
- Keigo mismatch: 尊敬語/謙譲語 appearing in neutral expository prose, or
  お/ご prefixes on technical nouns.

## Vocabulary and phrasing tells

- Katakana buzzword density: ソリューション、インサイト、シナジー、
  エコシステム、ベストプラクティス. Use the established Japanese term when
  one exists (知見、相乗効果、事例).
- Stiff Sino-Japanese verbs where plain verbs read naturally: 実施する →
  行う/やる, 活用する → 使う, 提供する → 出す (register permitting).
- Bloated potential forms: 「〜することができます」 → 「〜できます」.
- Defensive sentence endings on every claim: 「〜と言えるでしょう」
  「〜ではないでしょうか」「〜かもしれません」. One softened claim is
  politeness; every claim softened is evasion. Let the author assert.
- 「〜において」「〜における」 translationese for で/の: 「日本において」
  → 「日本で」 unless the register is legal or academic.
- Mechanical paragraph openers: また、さらに、一方で、なお on every
  paragraph. Rely on content order; keep connectives for real turns.

## Structure and rhythm tells

- 体言止め either absent entirely (monotone) or used every other sentence
  (listicle cadence). Sprinkle it for rhythm in blogs and copy; avoid it in
  formal prose.
- Uniform sentence length; natural Japanese alternates short assertions with
  longer explanatory sentences.
- Rule-of-three noun chains (「速く、簡単で、信頼できる」) recurring as the
  default emphasis pattern.
- Triple dots and dashes (三点リーダー「…」、ダッシュ「――」) as dramatic
  pauses in expository text; prefer ending the sentence.

## Punctuation and format tells

- Mixing 、。 with half-width commas/periods; body text uses 、。 with
  half-width only inside code, numbers, and Latin names.
- Bold lead-in bullet lists substituting for paragraphs; prose first unless
  items are genuinely parallel.
- Spaces inserted around full-width and half-width boundaries inconsistently;
  pick one convention (commonly no space) and hold it.
